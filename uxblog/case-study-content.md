## CASE STUDY 01 — CARD RITUAL

**Company:** Card Ritual
**Overview:** A tarot journaling web app designed for practitioners who have outgrown every passive app they've tried. Built solo in three months — concept, design, development, AI integration, and brand.
**Year:** 2026
**Role:** Founder, Designer & Developer
**Scope:** Product Design · AI Integration · SaaS · Brand Strategy · Content

---

**[IMAGE: Hero]**
Label: Hero image
Hint: Full product screenshot, app open, card pull interface front and center. Clean, atmospheric. Let the UI speak.

---

**Challenge:**
Existing tarot apps are passive. They give you a card and a definition and stop there. Nothing supports the reflection, pattern recognition, or decision-making that makes a daily practice actually useful. The tools didn't match how serious practitioners use tarot.

**Solution:**
A journaling-first web app with AI as a thought partner, not an answer machine. Every feature wraps around one core loop: pull, reflect, record, revisit. Built solo from concept to production in three months.

---

**[IMAGE: Process]**
Label: Process / early iterations
Hint: Early wireframes or Figma flows — show the core loop mapped out before any polish. Whiteboard photo works too.

---

**Feature 01**
Headline: Nine years of personal practice as the only user research that mattered.
Body: Starting from my own daily ritual meant I knew exactly where every existing tool failed. The journaling loop was designed from lived experience, not assumption. That's a different kind of product clarity than any focus group delivers.

**[IMAGE: Feature 01]**
Label: Feature 01 image
Hint: The journaling interface — entry field, card display, or a real in-app entry. Show the moment of reflection.

---

**Feature 02**
Headline: AI as a thought partner, not an answer machine.
Body: The prompt architecture is the product. Instead of generating interpretations, the AI surfaces questions — preserving the human layer that makes tarot useful as a decision-making tool. The meaning always belongs to the user.

**[IMAGE: Feature 02]**
Label: Feature 02 image
Hint: The AI interaction screen — the question it asks, the response field, the moment before the user types.

---

**Industry Challenge:**
The tarot app market is saturated with passive, single-session tools. No product has successfully bridged serious spiritual practice with longitudinal journaling. Most apps are built by developers who don't practice — and it shows.

**[IMAGE: Industry Challenge]**
Label: Competitive landscape
Hint: A side-by-side teardown of 3–4 competitors. Screenshots in a grid. Label what's missing in each.

**Opportunity:**
Card Ritual targets the practitioner who has outgrown every app they've tried. By designing around continuity — returning to past entries, tracking emotional patterns, watching meaning evolve — the product fills a gap no competitor is trying to close.

---

**Feature 03**
Headline: An App Store rejection became a better product decision.
Body: Hitting Apple's wall mid-launch forced a web-first pivot. Faster iteration, no gatekeeping, immediate cross-device access. What looked like a setback was a cleaner path to ship — and the product is stronger for it.

**[IMAGE: Feature 03]**
Label: Feature 03 image
Hint: The experience across multiple devices — responsive design working on mobile, tablet, desktop. Stack the screens.

---

**Feature 04**
Headline: A content strategy built around a real desert and a real practice.
Body: Daily card pulls filmed in the Sonoran Desert — living the day, then documenting how the card showed up. It's not content about tarot. It's tarot lived in public. Non-follower reach outperformed benchmarks from week one.

**[IMAGE: Feature 04]**
Label: Feature 04 image
Hint: Behind-the-scenes desert shoot — the card, the environment, the light. Raw and real.

---

**Challenges & Obstacles:**
Building alone is brutal honesty. App Store rejection mid-launch. Trial users converting but retention requiring a full drip sequence rebuild. No team to pressure-test decisions — just the product and whether it actually works. That accountability made every design choice sharper.

**[IMAGE: Supporting]**
Label: Supporting image
Hint: The tech stack — Stripe dashboard, Supabase schema, or your stack laid out visually. Show what one person built.

---

**Conclusion:**
Card Ritual is the clearest expression of everything I believe about design — that your best work comes from a problem you actually live inside. The app is live, the content is running, and the practice that inspired it is nine years deep.

**Outcome:**
- 3 months — Concept to production, built solo
- Live — Shipped and active with real users
- Web-first — Pivoted from App Store to stronger distribution
- Trial-to-paid drip sequence running
- Content reaching beyond followers from week one

## CASE STUDY 02 — GREEN COMPASS

**Company:** Green Compass
**Overview:** As Director of Digital Experience, I led all aspects of the digital customer journey for a growing CBD wellness company — enrollment design, branding, email marketing, video production, and back office systems — in one of the most regulated industries in e-commerce.
**Year:** 2024
**Role:** Director of Digital Experience
**Scope:** UX/UI Design · Enrollment Flows · Brand Direction · Email Marketing · Content Production

---

**[IMAGE: Hero]**
Label: Hero image
Hint: The Balance and Restore enrollment flow — a clean multi-step screen showing the bundle-building experience.

---

**Challenge:**
Green Compass needed a full-funnel digital experience in an industry where you can't make health claims, payment processors are skeptical, and every state has different CBD shipping laws. Enrollment flows, branding, marketing, and back office systems all needed to work together — compliantly.

**Solution:**
Own the entire digital experience as a director-level function — not just design deliverables, but strategy, creative direction, production, and cross-functional leadership across compliance, marketing, and development.

---

**[IMAGE: Process]**
Label: Process / early iterations
Hint: Early enrollment flow sketches or a whiteboard session — the multi-step logic mapped out before it was designed. If you have a flow diagram, use it.

---

**Feature 01**
Headline: Designing complexity out of a deeply complex enrollment system.
Body: The Balance and Restore bundle builder had to handle product restrictions, state-by-state shipping limits, age verification, autoship setup, FDA disclaimers, and distributor relationships — all without overwhelming a user mid-purchase. Progressive disclosure was the design principle that made it work: show the right rule at the right moment, not all of them at once.

**[IMAGE: Feature 01]**
Label: Feature 01 image
Hint: The multi-step enrollment flow — show two or three steps side by side. Bundle selection, upsell moment, compliance checkpoint.

---

**Feature 02**
Headline: Brand direction across every touchpoint, not just the website.
Body: Email campaigns, social content, product photography direction, video editing, conference videos, trade show materials — all of it maintained a cohesive visual identity. That consistency didn't happen by accident. It happened because one person held the creative standard across all of them.

**[IMAGE: Feature 02]**
Label: Feature 02 image
Hint: A spread of brand touchpoints — email, social post, product photo, and a web screen. Show the range.

---

**Industry Challenge:**
CBD is the rare industry where your biggest design challenge is legal, not creative. FDA regulations restrict health claims. Platform advertising bans limit reach. State laws create a fragmented compliance map that changes constantly. Designers who haven't worked in this space underestimate how much of the UX is actually legal strategy.

**[IMAGE: Industry Challenge]**
Label: Competitive / regulatory landscape
Hint: A visual showing the regulatory complexity — state restriction map, or a simplified diagram of the compliance layers involved in a single purchase.

**Opportunity:**
In a market where most brands lean into vague wellness language and hope for the best, Green Compass committed to transparency and compliance as a design value. That's the positioning that builds durable trust — and the products and flows I designed made that trust legible at every touchpoint.

---

**Feature 03**
Headline: Heuristic analysis as an ongoing design practice, not a one-time audit.
Body: Regular evaluation of site usability, identification of friction points, and iteration cycles meant the digital experience improved continuously — not just at launch. That discipline is what keeps a fast-growing company from accumulating UX debt.

**[IMAGE: Feature 03]**
Label: Feature 03 image
Hint: A before/after comparison — a screen that improved after analysis, annotated to show what changed and why.

---

**Feature 04**
Headline: Video and photography production that kept the brand coherent across channels.
Body: Directed product photography, edited promotional and conference videos, and created social content — all while maintaining the visual standards established for the brand. Wearing this many hats isn't about doing them all adequately. It's about understanding how they feed each other.

**[IMAGE: Feature 04]**
Label: Feature 04 image
Hint: Product photography setup or a video production still — show the work behind the work.

---

**Challenges & Obstacles:**
The CBD payment processing landscape was a constant constraint — certain processors decline outright, others impose restrictions that affect checkout design. Compliance review on every piece of copy slowed iteration. And building a cohesive brand while operating as a one-person creative department meant ruthless prioritization was the skill underneath everything else.

**[IMAGE: Supporting]**
Label: Supporting image
Hint: The back office interface or campaign dashboard — show the operational side of the work.

---

**Conclusion:**
Green Compass proved that regulated industries don't require ugly UX — they require smarter UX. The constraint of compliance, handled well, becomes a design advantage. Trust is visible when you build it deliberately into every interaction.

**Outcome:**
- Launched compliant, conversion-optimized enrollment flow with complex business rules
- Directed cohesive brand across email, social, web, and video
- Established visual identity guidelines that scaled with company growth
- Reduced customer service load through improved back office UX

## CASE STUDY 03 — PETSMART

**Company:** PetSmart
**Overview:** As Senior Web Designer, I led UX improvements across PetSmart.com — a million-plus-page e-commerce platform — using A/B testing, heat mapping, and user behavior analysis. I redesigned the homepage, global navigation, and product detail pages, launched the Puppy Experience, and managed the Universal Studios Secret Life of Pets partnership, all while coordinating a complete platform migration.
**Year:** 2015
**Role:** Senior Web Designer
**Scope:** UX/UI Design · E-Commerce · A/B Testing · Brand Partnerships · Platform Migration

---

**[IMAGE: Hero]**
Label: Hero image
Hint: The PetSmart homepage redesign — your cleanest design output from the project. If you have a before/after, use it.

---

**Challenge:**
Redesigning critical pages on a million-plus-page platform where any change multiplies across massive traffic. The homepage — the most visited page on the entire site — needed to serve dog owners, cat owners, bird owners, fish owners, and reptile people simultaneously. And the whole platform was migrating to a new tech stack mid-project.

**Solution:**
Data-driven design at scale. Every decision validated through A/B tests, heat maps, and behavior analysis before rollout. Close coordination with the in-house dev team throughout the migration. Regular executive presentations to keep design decisions resourced and moving.

---

**[IMAGE: Process]**
Label: Process / data
Hint: A/B test results or heat map overlays — the data behind a design decision. This is the process image that proves you don't guess.

---

**Feature 01**
Headline: Redesigning the highest-traffic page on a million-page site.
Body: The PetSmart homepage wasn't just a design problem — it was a responsibility. Highest traffic. Most visible. Every pixel change measured. I redesigned the architecture and layout using heat map engagement data, tested variations against live traffic, and maintained the page with continuous improvement after launch.

**[IMAGE: Feature 01]**
Label: Feature 01 image
Hint: Homepage before/after, or a single strong final-state screen. Annotate what changed and why.

---

**Feature 02**
Headline: Transforming global navigation for a catalog that never stops growing.
Body: Traditional mega-menus failed at PetSmart's product breadth. I restructured the navigation hierarchy based on how users actually moved through the site — not how merchandising wished they would. Simplified category organization, improved mobile nav, and created clear paths to the highest-converting sections.

**[IMAGE: Feature 02]**
Label: Feature 02 image
Hint: The navigation redesign — show the before/after or annotate the new hierarchy. Mobile and desktop if you have both.

---

**Industry Challenge:**
Enterprise e-commerce at this scale is a fundamentally different design discipline. A small navigation change affects millions of page views. A product detail page template covers thousands of SKUs. Design systems aren't optional at this scale — they're the only way to maintain quality when the platform is too large for any human to audit manually.

**[IMAGE: Industry Challenge]**
Label: Scale diagram
Hint: Visualize what "one million pages" means. Or a breakdown of page types to show the scope of any single design decision.

**Opportunity:**
PetSmart had the traffic. What it needed was a homepage and navigation that converted that traffic into deeper engagement. With data as the design foundation, every improvement was defensible — to users, to executives, and to the platform migration team.

---

**Feature 03**
Headline: The Puppy Experience — designing for a life event, not a shopping session.
Body: New puppy owners don't want a product catalog. They want help. I designed a dedicated hub with age-appropriate product recommendations, educational content, curated bundles, and journey-based navigation — first week, first month, first year. It turned PetSmart into a resource, not just a store.

**[IMAGE: Feature 03]**
Label: Feature 03 image
Hint: The Puppy Experience hub — show the entry point and one or two interior screens. The journey-based navigation is the story.

---

**Feature 04**
Headline: Managing a high-profile brand partnership with Universal Studios.
Body: The Secret Life of Pets integration required working directly with Universal's brand team, navigating their IP requirements, coordinating across PetSmart legal, marketing, and merchandising, and delivering on a hard movie-release deadline. Two brand identities. One seamless campaign. No blown timelines.

**[IMAGE: Feature 04]**
Label: Feature 04 image
Hint: The Secret Life of Pets campaign landing page or promotional materials — show the co-branded execution.

---

**Challenges & Obstacles:**
A complete platform migration mid-project is the kind of constraint that breaks timelines. Coordinating design requirements with a dev team rebuilding the foundation underneath you requires constant communication, willingness to adapt, and the ability to distinguish what absolutely can't move from what's actually flexible.

**[IMAGE: Supporting]**
Label: Supporting image
Hint: An executive presentation slide or a design spec document — show the professional artifact that moved decisions forward in a large organization.

---

**Conclusion:**
Enterprise design taught me that scale changes everything. Not just technically — strategically. The decisions that matter most at a million-page platform aren't the aesthetic ones. They're the systems ones. The ones that hold up when no one is looking.

**Outcome:**
- Redesigned the highest-traffic page on a million-plus-page platform
- Transformed global navigation for millions of monthly users
- Launched the Puppy Experience and Secret Life of Pets campaign
- Maintained design quality through a complete platform migration
- Moved to data-driven design as standard practice across the team

## CASE STUDY 04 — PLEXUS WORLDWIDE

**Company:** Plexus Worldwide
**Overview:** As the first UX/UI Designer at Plexus Worldwide, I built the digital design function from scratch — establishing the design system, bridging design and development, and leading three major platform redesigns across e-commerce, an ambassador site builder, and a back-office dashboard serving thousands of users.
**Year:** 2017
**Role:** UX/UI Designer (Pioneering Role)
**Scope:** UX/UI Design · Design Systems · Front-End Development · Platform Architecture · Direct Sales UX

---

**[IMAGE: Hero]**
Label: Hero image
Hint: The most polished output from the third redesign — e-commerce checkout, dashboard, or the site builder. Show the system at its best.

---

**Challenge:**
No design function. No design system. No shared language between marketing and development. A direct sales platform with legally complex compensation rules, Exigo API constraints, thousands of ambassador websites to generate, and a business growing faster than its digital infrastructure.

**Solution:**
Build everything. Start with process, not pixels. Establish the atomic design system first so that every redesign after it could move faster, stay consistent, and bridge the design-to-development gap that was costing the company time and money.

---

**[IMAGE: Process]**
Label: Process / design system architecture
Hint: The Sketch component library or the atomic design hierarchy laid out — atoms, molecules, organisms, templates. Show the system architecture.

---

**Feature 01**
Headline: Three redesigns, each one smarter than the last.
Body: The first redesign set the baseline. The second incorporated A/B test data and ambassador feedback. The third implemented the full design system with Angular component parity. None of them started from scratch — each one was strategic evolution built on what we learned from real users and real data.

**[IMAGE: Feature 01]**
Label: Feature 01 image
Hint: A side-by-side of redesign generations — show the visible progression. Even two versions is a compelling before/after.

---

**Feature 02**
Headline: A design system that improved handoff efficiency by 60%.
Body: The atomic design system wasn't just a Sketch library — it was a shared language. Atoms became Angular components. Design tokens became SCSS variables. The result was a mapping so direct that developers could implement designs without re-interpreting them. That's the kind of efficiency that compounds across every future project.

**[IMAGE: Feature 02]**
Label: Feature 02 image
Hint: The component library — show atoms through organisms, or the Sketch-to-Angular mapping side by side.

---

**Industry Challenge:**
Direct sales UX is uniquely hostile to simplicity. Exigo's API imposes structural constraints. Compensation plans generate business logic that touches every transaction. State and federal regulations restrict what you can say and how. Designing for this environment means designing within a web of rules that most UX patterns don't account for.

**[IMAGE: Industry Challenge]**
Label: Platform complexity diagram
Hint: A diagram of the platform complexity — how many systems touch a single enrollment or purchase. Exigo, ambassador layers, compliance, autoship. Show what you were designing against.

**Opportunity:**
Most direct sales companies treat their digital tools as administrative necessities. Plexus had the chance to make those tools a genuine competitive advantage — giving ambassadors better data, a better site-building experience, and a back office they actually wanted to use. That's what drives retention in direct sales.

---

**Feature 03**
Headline: An ambassador site builder that empowered thousands of non-technical users.
Body: Every Plexus ambassador needed a personal website. I designed a replicated site system with controlled personalization — templates with real customization, brand guardrails built in, visual preview, and SEO optimization. Non-technical users could build a professional web presence without touching code or calling support.

**[IMAGE: Feature 03]**
Label: Feature 03 image
Hint: The site builder interface — show the template selection, the customization options, and a finished ambassador site. Walk through the system.

---

**Feature 04**
Headline: A ZenDesk integration suggestion that cut the customer service team by 60%.
Body: I noticed that the most common support contacts were answerable through better self-service design. I proposed a custom-themed ZenDesk integration with structured knowledge base architecture. The company implemented it — and reduced their customer service headcount by 60%. Design thinking applied to operations isn't overhead. It's leverage.

**[IMAGE: Feature 04]**
Label: Feature 04 image
Hint: The ZenDesk help center — the custom theme, the knowledge base structure, the search interface. Show it as a product.

---

**Challenges & Obstacles:**
Exigo's API limitations shaped what was technically possible — and not always in design-friendly ways. Compensation plan logic added constraints that surfaced mid-build. And pioneering a design function means every process you create is being invented without a precedent to follow. The work practices I established are still in use at Plexus today because there was no other choice but to build them right the first time.

**[IMAGE: Supporting]**
Label: Supporting image
Hint: The back-office dashboard — commissions view, team genealogy, order management. The most complex screen shows the depth of the design work.

---

**Conclusion:**
Pioneering a design function means building infrastructure — not just interfaces. The processes outlast any single project. The design system multiplies every future effort. The cross-team language you create becomes the way the company works. That's the difference between a designer and a design practice.

**Outcome:**
- Built Plexus Worldwide's first digital design function from zero
- Led three major platform redesigns, each building on the last
- Improved design-to-development handoff efficiency by 60%
- Enabled a 60% reduction in customer service staffing through ZenDesk integration
- Established work practices still used by both marketing and development departments today
